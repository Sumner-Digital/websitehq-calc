# WebsiteHQ ROI Calculator — Sales Page Writer Brief

> **Purpose:** Give the copywriter a complete understanding of the ROI calculator so they can write sales page copy that works hand-in-hand with it. The calculator sells the $125/mo WebsiteHQ managed WordPress hosting service.

---

## What the Calculator Does (Big Picture)

The calculator lets a WordPress business owner plug in their REAL numbers — what they pay for hosting, security, backups, speed tools, and how many hours they burn every month managing their site. It then compares that total (cash + time) against WebsiteHQ's flat **$125/mo** price.

**The core insight it exploits:** Most WordPress owners have NO IDEA how much they're actually spending when you add up all the separate tools AND their time. The calculator makes the invisible visible.

### Section 1: "WordPress Monthly Bills"

Cash costs people pay separately and forget to add up:

| Field | Default | What It Covers |
|-------|---------|----------------|

**For the writer:** People don't think of these as one bill. They see $50 here, $20 there. The calculator forces them to see the REAL combined total for the first time.

| Field | Default | What It Covers |

## 3 Smart Display Modes (The Calculator Adapts Automatically)

The calculator reads the user's inputs and displays one of THREE different result experiences. This is critical for the writer to understand — no matter what numbers the visitor enters, the calculator has a compelling message ready.

---

### Mode 1: "You're Saving Money" *(Most visitors will see this)*

**Triggers when:** Their WordPress costs exceed $125/mo (which happens with defaults)

**What they see:**
- Big green banner: **"You're Wasting $8,820 Every Year"**
- Below it: "That's $735 every month you could be saving"
- Stat cards: WordPress annual cost vs. WebsiteHQ annual cost vs. hours saved
- Time callout showing hours saved: "No more updates, troubleshooting, or fighting with WordPress"

**For the writer:** This is the happy path. The savings number is big, bold, and undeniable because it came from their own inputs. The word "wasting" (not "saving") creates urgency through loss aversion.

---

### Mode 2: "Your Site Is Exposed" *(Fear-based, for cheapskate visitors)*

**Triggers when:** They spend very little on WordPress AND have no security, no backups, or don't update plugins

**What they see:**
- Big red banner: **"Your Site Is Exposed"**
- Scary number: **"One hack could cost $3,000–$5,000"**
- Below it: "You're spending less because you have no protection in place"
- Red stat cards: "30,000+ Sites Hacked Daily" / "$427/min Avg. Downtime Cost" / "60% Of Businesses Close After Attack"
- Green pitch: "WebsiteHQ protects your business for just $125/mo"

**For the writer:** This catches the person who proudly says "I only spend $20/mo on hosting." The calculator flips their confidence into fear — you're cheap because you're UNPROTECTED. The hack recovery cost ($3K–$5K) reframes $125/mo as affordable insurance.

---

### Mode 3: "Value Swap" *(For visitors who already invest in good tools)*

**Triggers when:** Savings are negative BUT they DO have security and backups (they're already spending on protection, just not enough to exceed $125/mo total)

**What they see:**
- Blue banner: **"Everything You Need, One Simple Plan"**
- Price: **"$125/mo"**
- Below it: "Speed, security, managed updates, and hassle-free editing — all included"
- Blue stat cards: "Managed Updates" / "Speed Optimized" / "Zero Maintenance"

**For the writer:** This person can't be scared or shown savings. Instead, the calculator sells SIMPLICITY — one bill, one provider, zero hassle. The pitch shifts from "save money" to "save headaches."

**For the writer:** The dismiss button is psychologically powerful — clicking "I Understand the Risks" means they're consciously choosing to stay unprotected. Most people won't feel comfortable doing that.

## What the Sales Page Copy Should Do

1. **Set up the pain BEFORE the calculator.** The sales page needs to introduce the WordPress hidden cost problem, the hacking risk, and the time drain. The calculator then confirms it with their own numbers. The calculator is the CLOSER, not the opener.

2. **Anchor on the true cost first.** By the time visitors see $125/mo, they've already seen $860+ as their true WordPress cost. Price anchoring is built into the calculator — the sales page should mirror this.

3. **Address all three audiences.** The calculator auto-adapts to three visitor types — the sales page should too:
   - **Overspenders** → "You're wasting money" (savings-focused copy)
   - **Budget users** → "You're exposed" (fear-focused copy about hacking)
   - **Tool-stacked users** → "Simplify everything" (convenience-focused copy)

4. **Use the same language.** The calculator says "wasting," "exposed," "ticking time bomb," "handled for you," "zero headaches." The sales page copy should use these same phrases to create consistency.

5. **The defaults are conservative.** $50 hosting, $50/hr time, 15 hours/month. These aren't inflated — the assumptions section says "conservative averages based on typical small business WordPress websites." The writer can confidently say the calculator uses conservative numbers.

6. **Sliders let them adjust everything.** If a visitor thinks 10 hours for plugins is too high, they can lower it. The savings STILL look compelling at much lower numbers. Even at just 5 total hours and $30/hr, the time cost alone is $150/mo — still more than WebsiteHQ's fee.

7. **Three conversion points exist.** The "Get Started with WebsiteHQ" CTA appears in: the warning modal, the results panel, and the bottom of the page. The sales page should drive traffic to the calculator knowing it has multiple CTA touchpoints built in.
